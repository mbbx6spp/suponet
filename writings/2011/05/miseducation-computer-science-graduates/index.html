The Miseducation of Computer Science Graduates (in US)

Introduction

This is a draft of my critiques (hopefully constructive) on the topic of Computer Science education in post-high school level institutions, specifically research universities (in the United States as this is with what I have experience).

I hope to point out systemic deficiencies as far as I see them, as someone who has hired Computer Science graduates for my consulting clients and soon (I hope) for my new business venture when I get all my funding ducks in a row. The purpose of this draft (so far) is to help sort through my thoughts on the issues at hand, and potentially offer constructive suggestions for educators to test out in the lecture hall or computer lab (after revising my statements to more accurately depict my suggestions). It is not to point the finger at specific people, those with certain job titles or students. We are all in this together.

I hope the draft isn't too rough for people to read through so they can provide feedback, because I want this to be interactive and constructive for everyone. Not simply a venting platform for me. If it is let me know.

Motivations

My motivations are, undeniably, selfish. They are:

  1. I want to be able to hire CS graduates that can be productive on day one instead of spending a good amount of time introducing them to aspects of software engineering I had hoped would be covered in their undergraduate degrees.
  2. I also want to continue being able to live in a country that wil be economically competitive such that at least some creative jobs remain here for the rest of my career instead of being forced to lift up my roots to relocate somewhere I may not care to live just to satisfy my professional goals (of course, I may choose instead to wither and die professionally in a less competitive economy where only boring support jobs exist). Neither of these potential outcomes appeal to me. If you are reading this from a developing economy thinking, "what an arrogant westerner" you would be correct. I haven't spent much of my life outside of western cultures, only the first seven years of it. As hard as it is to see a westerners point of view at least acknowledge that if you were in my shoes and had started your career in more creative, innovative positions that the you would also not look forward to a career that ends with dull, unchallenging support roles as I should be increasing in position, title, and salary if previous career trajectories are anything to go by. (Of course, they aren't but that is another matter entirely.)
  3. As a new parent now I hope educational reform will take place to better equip my child in the future.
