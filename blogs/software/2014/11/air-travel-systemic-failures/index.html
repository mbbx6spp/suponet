Air Travel Systememic Failures

%h3 Qualifications: Who The Hell Are You To Criticize? %p An exceptionally fair question, so let me talk about the limitations of my qualifications as I attempt to dissect the problems I experienced: %ul %li I have never worked for an airline in any capacity %li I have never worked for an airport in any capacity %li I haven't researched this space thoroughly (yet) from a business process and systems perspective %li I can only guess what the behind the scenes constraints and limitations are for a behemoth airline like American Airlines %p So judge this content considering the above. Correct me where I am wrong, or have made invalid assumptions. Tell me why because I would love to know more. %p As mentioned I am not an expert in airline business or travel industry. I am however a higher frequency than average air traveler (for both business and personal). Let me quantify this for transparency. In the last 12 months: %ul %li I have made one international round trip consisting of 4 legs of travel. %li I have made eleven domestic (US) round trips to four different destinations with typically four legs of travel each roundtrip. %li One trip was a multi-leg trip to two different US destinations over the two weeks. %li Two of these round trips were for personal purposes. The rest business. %li Most trips were from the midwest (my home local airport of CMI) to major west coast airports. One was from CMI (through ORD) to LGA (east coast). %li Most connections were through ORD. At least one was through DFW. %li Most of these legs were via American Airlines or a subsidiary/partner. %li At least four of these trips had weather related delays which are not considered as failures of the airline. However, the way they respond to external failure can be a failure. When a datacenter or router goes out, you need to figure out what the response is and you need to do this before it becomes reality. Users of your service and hosted solutions do not care that one of your partners lost power and didn't have the power redundancy they advertized. They just don't. Respond and care about how your respond. Shit happens, but...have a professional response to customers. %p %em Note: I am by no means a travel warrior, but I am not an irregular nor infrequent air traveler. Now you can assess how you should judge this content at least. %p Another qualification (I think) is that I am a software engineer who has designed and evolved various distributed systems (software) applied to a number of domains. This may not seem pertinent to many however I suggest it is relevant in the sense that I understand systems design in a much more abstract, theoretic but also practical and visceral way than many other passengers. For example, not only do I design these systems, I have been on call for them and woken up at 4am for all kinds of problems and severities. %p When I arrive at an airport to start my journey, or when I am waiting at an airport between flights (connecting), boarding, deplaning, collecting baggage (valet or claim), or leaving the airport (ground transport) I see systems and processes that are connecting people, baggage, and places. I can see the implications of queuing theory - something I have to know for my job to initially design, understand, troubleshoot, and tune software systems - on airport, security, and airline procedures. %p I have previously thought it would be great to write a book/paper/series of blog posts/articles on parallels between software design and runtime characteristics under loads or security breaches and air travel under such conditions. This is just how my mind works. I love this kind of stuff. %p I am probably over simplifying some of what I will write in this series. I am writing in haste to get something out there and show I am interested in providing constructive feedback now that my anger and frustrations as a passenger have subsided from these experiences. Talk to me. Tell me what logical errors or problematic assumptions I have made about the systemic issues I perceived as a passenger only seeing one side of it. %p I welcome the feedback. Always. %h3 The Good %h4 In-flight Experience %p It's not all bad. Some of the legs and even a couple of roundtrips I have had zero significant issues. Most of my in-flight experiences have been more than tolerable, some were even enjoyable. Most of the time gate and flight attendant staff are professional, courteous, fulfilling their intended role, and helpful. %h4 Mobile App %p I am enjoying the new improvements to the American Airlines mobile app, which now include some (limited, but some) metrics on delays of that flight. It even specifies the context bounds of these metrics making the metrics reported actually meaningful. Thanks for this! It also now includes the upstream flight that provides the aircraft. I will talk more about why this is valuable as a passenger in my tips for passengers post. %h3 Context %p My experience is probably some of the worst experienced by American Airlines by virtue of being based out of the CMI local airport. Why? It only has connection flights to two major hubs: ORD and DFW. According to a report BLA BLA (need to cite source here) these are the two "worst" airports for delays and cancelations in the whole of the US. %p Having said that, as a passenger, in-flight, I am pretty low fuss. I typically travel alone (I haven't flown with the kids in the last twelve months, but know this will change over new year:(). I am fully able bodied and I do not make a fuss about most things. I am almost always willing to take the crappiest seat (based on what many consider desireable features) just to be sure I am on the flights I expect to be on. I do not call the attendants and ask for extra things or anything like that. I don't try to game the system for the "best seats" or try to jump ahead of people before my boarding group's turn. I am also almost never late to the gate (except for bad/delayed connections). i.e. other passengers aren't waiting for me. %h3 Scope %p There are so many processes, procedures, and systemic failures and how I could suggest improvements in the air travel industry and there are actually many, much more well versed experts that intimately understand the problems technically and practically than I do. This series of posts is limited to suggesting improvements or highlighting high frustration points that could be isolated, managed (expecations), or removed complete from the below mentioned sub-posts. %h3 Dissecting the problems %p To attempt to address some of the concerns and provide constructive and hopefully meaningful suggestions for improvement I am going to separate the posts to address specific parts of the problem. %p Here is my first cut at the dissection of the whole experience: %ul %li %a{ :href => "/blogs/software/2014/11/air-travel-systemic-failures/gate-seat-assignments/"} Exposing internal processes to impact certain passengers negatively (plus "bugs") %li %a{ :href => "/blogs/software/2014/11/air-travel-systemic-failures/travel-experience-connections/"} In-flight experience and connection process improvements (good and bad) %li %a{ :href => "/blogs/software/2014/11/air-travel-systemic-failures/overbooking/"} Double- or over- booking seats on flights (suggestions to reduce passenger frustration and possibly even out demand/load on flights) %li %a{ :href => "/blogs/software/2014/11/air-travel-systemic-failures/customer-service-suggestions/"} Suggestions for American Airlines on customer service %li %a{ :href => "/blogs/software/2014/11/air-travel-systemic-failures/passenger-tips/" } Tips for other passengers to reduce your steps based on what I now know
