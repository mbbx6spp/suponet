Command-line Usability

2014-10-22 18:56:00 -0700

Why Command-line Usability Matters

The short answer is because you will enrage the people that use the scripts you have written. These people are likely already frustrated with their work activities, why inflict more pain and suffering on the world?

Another, perhaps more compelling (to some) reason is that you might need to use your own scripts one day. And you might be sorry. Very sorry if you didn't think of command-line usability from the outset.

The better we, as a profession, improve our focus on all aspects of usability the less irritating, less stressful, and more productive our day-to-day work existence and balance could become. Isn't that something to strive for?

The VM Provisioning Script from the Darkest Abyss of Hell

I came to writing this post because I had to provision some virtual machines at work for our new derived metrics monitoring solution. I had previously used the script for VM provisioning months before and almost forgot how insane it was.

And then I started provisioning...

I died a little inside each minute that passed. Not only was the process slow, it involved many steps, it had to be run from one specific host on your network, which wasn't in a code reviewed repository or maintained by any team or collective of relevantly skilled people in the organization (read: management failure).

It was a script initially written in anger, rewritten in frustration and then tweaked by many station chefs without an eye for the bigger picture or how all the peices should fit together. There was no sous chef let along a qualified head chef with the grander vision in mind for the dish.

Let me stress one thing. This script and all of the scripts it depended on was not maanged as living, evolving software in any kind of source repository used within the organization. It was just a random sequence of bytes on a filesystem under /opt/vmware/bin. I will come back to that later, but for now if you aren't horrified by this notion, then you should meditate on it or reconsider your profession. Consider the implications of software entropy when software is totally unmanaged like this not to mention the organizational risks of potentially losing automation because of a corrupt disk.

Now in this case I am not 100% sure losing the "automated" VM provisioning script would have been a bad thing assuming there was (a) a team responsible for such things and held accountable for delivering on such a promise and (b) that team was actually capable of delivering a worthwhile automation for VM provisioning.

I cannot comment on whether this was or wasn't the case here. The rule of it being better to lose heartbreakingly shoddy code when you have the relevant team, responsibilities, skillset assigned, and culture setup in your organization to rewrite something of limited scope pretty much always holds. I stand by this. Fresh canvases after learning experiences can be both therapeutic and a force for great good and improvement.

Now this would not have been as traumatic an experience if the script automation itself didn't violate some basic tenets of the most basic script writing commandments. So let's look at the problems so we can better understand usability.
